- ## **Q1 — Denavit–Hartenberg (D–H) Convention Proof (30 pts)**
  
  We are given two frames (frame 0 and frame 1) with a homogeneous transform $$A$$ mapping coordinates of frame 1 into frame 0. Frames satisfy:
- (DH1) x_1 \perp z_0.
- (DH2) x_1 intersects z_0.
  
  We must prove existence and uniqueness of parameters \theta,a,d,\alpha such that
  
  $$
  
  A = R_{z,\theta},T_{z,d},T_{x,a},R_{x,\alpha},
  
  $$
  
  and the product equals the standard D–H matrix (given in HW2).
  
  For convenience denote the 4×4 matrix product (as in the statement) and its components. The product evaluates to the standard form:
  
  $$
  
  A(\theta,a,d,\alpha)=
  
  \begin{bmatrix}
  
  \cos\theta & -\sin\theta\cos\alpha & \sin\theta\sin\alpha & a\cos\theta\[6pt]
  
  \sin\theta & \cos\theta\cos\alpha  & -\cos\theta\sin\alpha& a\sin\theta\\[6pt]
  
  0          & \sin\alpha              & \cos\alpha           & d\\[6pt]
  
  0 & 0 & 0 & 1
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  \tag{DH-matrix}
  
  $$
  
  Below we first give a constructive existence proof, then a uniqueness proof (explicit formulae showing parameters are determined by entries of \(A\)).
  
  (1) Existence of \(\theta,a,d,\alpha\) (15 pts)
  
  Idea (constructive): starting from frame 0, we apply the four elementary transforms in order to reach frame 1. Because of the geometric constraints (DH1, DH2), these four simple transforms are sufficient to align axes and origins.
  
  Step 0 — geometric picture. Let origin of frame 0 be \(O_0\) and of frame 1 be \(O_1\). Let \(z_0\) be the \(z\)-axis of frame 0 and \(x_1\) the \(x\)-axis of frame 1. By (DH2) line \(x_1\) intersects line \(z_0\) at some point \(P\). By (DH1) \(x_1\) is perpendicular to \(z_0\), so \(P\) is the unique intersection point.
  
  Construct the following sequence of operations that maps frame0 → frame1:
  
  1.	Rotate about \(z_0\) by angle \(\theta\) so that the rotated \(x_0\) aligns in direction with the line through \(P\) parallel to \(x_1\). This is \(R_{z,\theta}\).
  
  2.	Translate along \(z_0\) by distance \(d\) so that the origin moves along \(z_0\) to the intersection point \(P\). This is \(T_{z,d}\).
  
  3.	Translate along the new \(x\)-axis (the rotated \(x_0\)) by distance \(a\) to reach the origin \(O_1\). This is \(T_{x,a}\). (Since \(x_1\) intersects \(z_0\) at \(P\) and is perpendicular, the translation along the rotated \(x\) reaches \(O_1\).)
  
  4.	Rotate about the new \(x\)-axis by angle \(\alpha\) to align the \(z\)-axis directions (so that the rotated \(z\) matches \(z_1\)). This is \(R_{x,\alpha}\).
  
  Because the sequence aligns axes and places the origin correctly, the composition produces frame 1 from frame 0. Therefore there exist real numbers \(\theta,a,d,\alpha\) realizing the transform. The result of the composition is precisely the DH matrix given above.
  
  (Geometric justification detail: the uniqueness of intersection \(P\) and orthogonality ensure the intermediate steps are well-defined and finite; translations along axes and rotations about axes preserve orthogonality properties required to match final axes.)
  
  Thus existence is established: there exists a 4-tuple \((\theta,a,d,\alpha)\) such that \(A\) equals the D–H product.
  
  (2) Uniqueness of \(\theta,a,d,\alpha\) (15 pts)
  
  Assume \(A\) is known and equals the DH-form matrix (DH-matrix). Denote the entries of \(A\) as
  
  $$
  
  A = \begin{bmatrix}
  
  r_{11} & r_{12} & r_{13} & p_x\\[4pt]
  
  r_{21} & r_{22} & r_{23} & p_y\\[4pt]
  
  r_{31} & r_{32} & r_{33} & p_z\\[4pt]
  
  0 & 0 & 0 & 1
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  Comparing \(A\) with the DH-matrix, we have the equalities:
  
  •	Row 3 (from DH-matrix): \(r_{31}=0,\ r_{32}=\sin\alpha,\ r_{33}=\cos\alpha,\ p_z = d.\)
  
  •	Row 1 and Row 2 components:
  
  $$r_{11}=\cos\theta,\quad r_{21}=\sin\theta,$$
  
  $$r_{12}=-\sin\theta\cos\alpha,\quad r_{22}=\cos\theta\cos\alpha,$$
  
  $$r_{13}=\sin\theta\sin\alpha,\quad r_{23}=-\cos\theta\sin\alpha.$$
  
  •	Position components:
  
  $$p_x=a\cos\theta,\quad p_y=a\sin\theta.$$
  
  From these equalities we can solve for the parameters in closed form:
  
  1.	\(d\) is directly read off:
  
  $$
  
  d = p_z.
  
  $$
  
  2.	\(\alpha\) is determined (up to the usual \(2\pi\) ambiguity) from the third-row rotation entries:
  
  $$
  
  \sin\alpha = r_{32},\qquad \cos\alpha = r_{33}.
  
  $$
  
  Thus a principal value is
  
  $$
  
  \alpha = \operatorname{atan2}(r_{32},\,r_{33}).
  
  $$
  
  (Because \(r_{32}^2 + r_{33}^2 = \sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha = 1\), the solution is consistent.)
  
  3.	\(\theta\) is determined from the first column:
  
  $$
  
  \cos\theta = r_{11},\qquad \sin\theta = r_{21}
  
  $$
  
  so
  
  $$
  
  \theta = \operatorname{atan2}(r_{21},\,r_{11}).
  
  $$
  
  (Again unique up to \(2\pi\) — the standard periodic ambiguity of angles.)
  
  4.	\(a\) from the position entries \(p_x,p_y\):
  
  $$
  
  a\cos\theta = p_x,\quad a\sin\theta = p_y.
  
  $$
  
  Squaring and summing:
  
  $$
  
  a^2(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)=p_x^2+p_y^2 \Rightarrow a=\sqrt{p_x^2+p_y^2}.
  
  $$
  
  The sign of \(a\) is determined by the chosen direction of \(x_1\); in the consistent D–H convention \(a\ge0\) (link length), so \(a=\sqrt{p_x^2+p_y^2}\). Equivalently \( \operatorname{atan2}(p_y,p_x)=\theta\) must hold, which is consistent with the \(\theta\) computed earlier.
  
  Remarks on uniqueness / degeneracies:
  
  •	The formulae above give unique values for \(d,a,\alpha,\theta\) except for the usual \(2\pi\) periodicity of angular parameters and possible sign conventions for \(a\).
  
  •	If \(\cos\theta=\sin\theta=0\) (impossible simultaneously) or if some rotation entries cause a loss of information (e.g., if \(\sin\alpha=0\) then \(\alpha=0\) or \(\pi\) giving sign ambiguity for some entries), these correspond to special alignments — but the D–H conditions (DH1,DH2) and the presence of nonzero structure in the matrix ensure the parameters are determined consistently.
  
  •	Explicitly: given \(A\) with block structure matching DH-matrix, the above formulas produce a unique set \((\theta,a,d,\alpha)\) under the standard range choices for \(\operatorname{atan2}\) (e.g., \(\theta\in(-\pi,\pi]\), \(\alpha\in(-\pi,\pi]\), \(a\ge0\) ). Thus uniqueness (modulo trivial \(2\pi\) periodicities) is established.
  
  Therefore both existence and uniqueness are proved.
  
  Q2 — 2-Link Planar Robotic Arm Jacobian Analysis (50 pts)
  
  Given: Planar 2-link arm with link lengths \(L_1,L_2\). End-effector position:
  
  $$
  
  x = L_1\cos\theta_1 + L_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2),
  
  \qquad
  
  y = L_1\sin\theta_1 + L_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2).
  
  $$
  
  We use \(L_1=L_2=1\) m and current angles \(\theta_1=45^\circ,\ \theta_2=30^\circ\).
  
  (1) Definition of the Jacobian \(J(\theta_1,\theta_2)\) (10 pts)
  
  Definition: For a manipulator, the Jacobian \(J(\theta)\) maps joint velocity vector \(\dot{\boldsymbol\theta}\) to end-effector linear velocity (and angular velocity where applicable). Here the end-effector linear velocity in the plane is:
  
  $$
  
  \begin{bmatrix}\dot x\\ \dot y\end{bmatrix} = J(\theta_1,\theta_2)\begin{bmatrix}\dot\theta_1\\\dot\theta_2\end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  •	Number of rows: 2 (Cartesian velocities \(x,y\)).
  
  •	Number of columns: 2 (two joint rates \(\dot\theta_1,\dot\theta_2\)).
  
  •	Rows significance: each row relates joint velocities to a single Cartesian velocity component (first row → \(\dot x\), second row → \(\dot y\)).
  
  •	Columns significance: each column gives the contribution of an individual joint rate to the end-effector linear velocity (column 1 → effect of \(\dot\theta_1\), column 2 → effect of \(\dot\theta_2\)).
  
  (2) Derive and compute the Jacobian (10 pts)
  
  Differentiate \(x,y\) w.r.t. \(\theta_1,\theta_2\):
  
  Compute partial derivatives:
  
  $$
  
  \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta_1} = -L_1\sin\theta_1 - L_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2),\quad
  
  \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta_2} = -L_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2),
  
  $$
  
  $$
  
  \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta_1} =  L_1\cos\theta_1 + L_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2),\quad
  
  \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta_2} =  L_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2).
  
  $$
  
  Thus the Jacobian is
  
  $$
  
  J(\theta_1,\theta_2)
  
  \begin{bmatrix}
  
  -\,L_1\sin\theta_1 - L_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2) & -\,L_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\\[6pt]
  
  \ \ L_1\cos\theta_1 + L_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2) & \ \ L_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  Now substitute \(L_1=L_2=1,\ \theta_1=45^\circ,\ \theta_2=30^\circ\).
  
  Useful trigonometric values:
  
  $$
  
  \sin45^\circ=\cos45^\circ=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\approx0.70710678,
  
  $$
  
  $$
  
  \theta_1+\theta_2=75^\circ,\quad
  
  \sin75^\circ=\sin(180^\circ-105^\circ)=\approx0.96592583,\quad
  
  \cos75^\circ\approx0.25881905.
  
  $$
  
  Evaluate entries:
  
  •	\(J_{11} = -\sin45^\circ - \sin75^\circ \approx -0.70710678 - 0.96592583 = -1.67303261.\)
  
  •	\(J_{12} = -\sin75^\circ \approx -0.96592583.\)
  
  •	\(J_{21} = \cos45^\circ + \cos75^\circ \approx 0.70710678 + 0.25881905 = 0.96592583.\)
  
  •	\(J_{22} = \cos75^\circ \approx 0.25881905.\)
  
  So numerically:
  
  $$
  
  J \approx
  
  \begin{bmatrix}
  
  -1.67303261 & -0.96592583\\[4pt]
  
  \ 0.96592583 & \ 0.25881905
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  (3) Pseudoinverse \(J^\dagger\) and Moore–Penrose properties (10 pts)
  
  Since \(J\) is a square \(2\times2\) matrix and its determinant is nonzero (we compute below), the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse equals the ordinary inverse:
  
  Determinant (general form). For general \(L_1,L_2\), compute
  
  $$
  
  \det J = L_1L_2\sin\theta_2.
  
  $$
  
  (This identity can be derived algebraically — see part (4) below.)
  
  With \(L_1=L_2=1\) and \(\theta_2=30^\circ\), we have
  
  $$
  
  \det J = 1\cdot 1 \cdot \sin 30^\circ = \tfrac12 = 0.5 \neq 0.
  
  $$
  
  Thus \(J\) is invertible and
  
  $$
  
  J^\dagger = J^{-1}.
  
  $$
  
  Compute \(J^{-1}\) by the standard 2×2 inverse formula:
  
  $$
  
  J^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det J}
  
  \begin{bmatrix}
  
  J_{22} & -J_{12}\\[4pt]
  
  •	J_{21} & J_{11}
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  Substitute numerical entries (determinant = 0.5):
  
  $$
  
  J^{-1} \approx 2\cdot
  
  \begin{bmatrix}
  
  0.25881905 & 0.96592583\\[4pt]
  
  -0.96592583 & -1.67303261
  
  \end{bmatrix}
  
  \begin{bmatrix}
  
  0.51763810 & 1.93185166\\[4pt]
  
  -1.93185166 & -3.34606522
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  Because \(J^\dagger = J^{-1}\), the Moore–Penrose identities hold:
  
  1.	\(J J^\dagger J = J J^{-1} J = J\).
  
  2.	\(J^\dagger J J^\dagger = J^{-1} J J^{-1} = J^{-1} = J^\dagger.\)
  
  3.	\((J J^\dagger)^T = (J J^{-1})^T = I^T = I = J J^\dagger.\)
  
  4.	\((J^\dagger J)^T = (J^{-1} J)^T = I^T = I = J^\dagger J.\)
  
  Thus all four Moore–Penrose properties in the problem statement are satisfied.
  
  (If \(J\) were rank-deficient, the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse would be computed as \(J^\dagger = J^T (J J^T)^{-1}\) for full row rank \(J\), or via SVD in general.)
  
  (4) When is the Jacobian singular? (20 pts)
  
  General determinant formula derivation.
  
  Compute determinant of the general Jacobian:
  
  Let \(s_1=\sin\theta_1,\ c_1=\cos\theta_1,\ s_{12}=\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2),\ c_{12}=\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)\). With general \(L_1,L_2\):
  
  $$
  
  J=\begin{bmatrix}
  
  •	L_1 s_1 - L_2 s_{12} & - L_2 s_{12}\\[4pt]
  
  \ \ L_1 c_1 + L_2 c_{12} & \ \ L_2 c_{12}
  
  \end{bmatrix}.
  
  $$
  
  Then
  
  \[
  
  \begin{aligned}
  
  \det J &= (- L_1 s_1 - L_2 s_{12})\cdot(L_2 c_{12}) - (-L_2 s_{12})\cdot(L_1 c_1 + L_2 c_{12})\\[4pt]
  
  &= -L_1L_2 s_1 c_{12} - L_2^2 s_{12} c_{12} + L_1 L_2 s_{12} c_1 + L_2^2 s_{12} c_{12}\\[4pt]
  
  &= L_1 L_2 \big( -s_1 c_{12} + s_{12} c_1 \big).
  
  \end{aligned}
  
  \]
  
  Use trig identity for sum angles:
  
  s_{12} = \sin(\theta_1+\theta_2) = s_1 c_2 + c_1 s_2,
  \quad
  c_{12} = c_1 c_2 - s_1 s_2.
  
  Compute the parenthesis:
  
  \begin{aligned}
  •	s_1 c_{12} + s_{12} c_1
  &= -s_1(c_1 c_2 - s_1 s_2) + (s_1 c_2 + c_1 s_2)c_1\\[4pt]
  &= -s_1 c_1 c_2 + s_1^2 s_2 + s_1 c_1 c_2 + c_1^2 s_2\\[4pt]
  &= (s_1^2 + c_1^2) s_2 = s_2.
  \end{aligned}
  
  Hence
  
  $$
  
  \boxed{\det J = L_1 L_2 \sin\theta_2.}
  
  $$
  
  **Singularity condition:** The Jacobian is singular if and only if \det J = 0. Given L_1,L_2\neq0, this happens exactly when
  
  $$
  
  \sin\theta_2 = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \theta_2 = k\pi\quad (k\in\mathbb{Z}).
  
  $$
  
  **Physical interpretation:**
- \theta_2 = 0: the second link is aligned (collinear) with the first link in the same direction (arm fully extended). Columns of J become linearly dependent → no instantaneous motion in some Cartesian directions can be produced by infinitesimal joint motions (loss of manipulability or directional control).
- \theta_2 = \pi: the second link folded back along the first (collinear but opposite direction). Again the arm is singular (elbow fully folded).
  
  Thus the manipulator has singularities whenever the two links are collinear (either fully extended or folded back).
  
  **Consequences at singularity:**
- The manipulator loses one degree of freedom for Cartesian velocity: the reachable instantaneous velocity space is only 1-dimensional (the rank of J drops from 2 to 1).
- Near-singular configurations lead to large joint velocities when attempting a given Cartesian velocity (ill-conditioning).

- ## **Q3 — 1-DOF Planar Robotic Arm Dynamics (20 pts)**
  
  Consider a single rigid link of length L and mass m rotating about a frictionless revolute joint at the base. Gravity g acts downward. Moment of inertia about center of mass:
  
  $$
  
  I_c=\frac{1}{12}mL^2.
  
  $$
  
  Let \theta(t) be the joint angle measured from the horizontal (or chosen reference); choose sign convention consistently.
  
  We will formulate dynamics via:
- Newton–Euler (balance of moments) — write torque required to produce motion about base, including inertia and gravity terms.
- Lagrangian method — derive equation using kinetic and potential energy.
  
  ---
- ### **(1) Newton–Euler formulation (10 pts)**
  
  Treat the link as a rigid body rotating about the base. Use rotational dynamics about the base point (pivot). Let \tau denote the torque applied at the joint (positive in direction of increasing \theta). We need the moment of inertia about the base I_{base}. By the parallel-axis theorem:
  
  $$
  
  I_{base} = I_c + m r^2,
  
  $$
  
  where r is the distance from link COM to the base. For a uniform rod of length L with pivot at an end, the COM is at distance r = \frac{L}{2}. Thus
  
  $$
  
  I_{base} = \frac{1}{12}mL^2 + m\left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^2
  
  = \frac{1}{12}mL^2 + \frac{1}{4}mL^2 = \frac{1}{3}mL^2.
  
  $$
  
  Newton–Euler (moment balance about the base) gives:
  
  $$
  
  \tau = I_{base},\ddot\theta + \tau_{grav},
  
  $$
  
  where \tau_{grav} is the torque produced by gravity about the base.
  
  Compute \tau_{grav}: gravitational force mg acts at COM downward; moment arm about base equals horizontal perpendicular distance from COM to the line of action. If \theta is angle measured from horizontal, the torque due to gravity tending to rotate the link is:
- If \theta measured from horizontal, the lever arm and sign should be carefully selected. More commonly let \theta be measured from the vertical; regardless, standard result for a planar rod pivoted at one end:
  
  Torque magnitude due to gravity:
  
  $$
  
  \tau_{grav} = m g \cdot \frac{L}{2} \sin\phi,
  
  $$
  
  where \phi is the angle between the rod and the vertical direction (so \sin\phi gives the horizontal offset). If \theta is measured from horizontal, relation is \phi = 90^\circ - \theta etc. To present a standard form, measure \theta from the vertical downward direction so that when \theta=0 the rod points down. Then the gravitational torque about the pivot is
  
  $$
  
  \tau_{grav} = m g , \frac{L}{2} \sin\theta.
  
  $$
  
  Choosing sign convention so that positive \tau produces increasing \theta, the equation becomes:
  
  $$
  
  \boxed{\ \tau = \frac{1}{3} m L^2 ,\ddot\theta + \frac{1}{2} m g L \sin\theta. \ }
  
  \tag{NE-equation}
  
  $$
  
  (If you use a different reference for \theta, the trigonometric factor may appear with a minus sign — be consistent with the chosen direction: the form above is standard when \theta measured from vertical and positive in the direction of increasing gravitational moment sign.)
  
  This is the Newton–Euler equation: applied torque equals rotational inertia times angular acceleration plus gravitational torque.
  
  ---
- ### **(2) Lagrangian formulation (10 pts)**
  
  **Step A — Kinetic energy **T**.**
  
  The link is a rigid body rotating about the base with angular velocity \dot\theta. Kinetic energy has two contributions: rotational kinetic energy about COM plus translational kinetic energy of COM; but easier is to compute rotational kinetic about base:
  
  Using I_{base}=\tfrac{1}{3}mL^2, kinetic energy
  
  $$
  
  T = \tfrac{1}{2} I_{base} \dot\theta^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3}mL^2,\dot\theta^2 = \frac{1}{6} m L^2 \dot\theta^2.
  
  $$
  
  (Alternatively compute COM velocity v_{COM}=(L/2)\dot\theta and rotational about COM \tfrac12 I_c\dot\theta^2, then sum: T=\tfrac12 m v_{COM}^2 + \tfrac12 I_c\dot\theta^2 = \tfrac12 m (L^2/4)\dot\theta^2 + \tfrac12(1/12 mL^2)\dot\theta^2 = (1/8 + 1/24) mL^2 \dot\theta^2 = (3/24+1/24) mL^2 \dot\theta^2 = \tfrac{1}{6}mL^2 \dot\theta^2. Same result.)
  
  **Step B — Potential energy **V**.**
  
  Choose reference V=0 when COM at base height zero or other convenient reference. If \theta measured from vertical downward, vertical position of COM is y_{COM} = (L/2)\cos\theta. Potential energy (with gravity pointing downward):
  
  $$
  
  V = m g y_{COM} = m g \frac{L}{2} \cos\theta.
  
  $$
  
  **Lagrangian **\mathcal{L} = T - V.
  
  $$
  
  \mathcal{L}(\theta,\dot\theta) = \frac{1}{6}mL^2\dot\theta^2 - m g \frac{L}{2}\cos\theta.
  
  $$
  
  **Euler–Lagrange equation:**
  
  $$
  
  \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot\theta}\right) - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} = \tau_{\text{ext}},
  
  $$
  
  where \tau_{\text{ext}} is the generalized torque input (actuator torque) acting as external generalized force.
  
  Compute:
- $$\dfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot\theta} = \dfrac{1}{3} m L^2 \dot\theta$$
- $$\dfrac{d}{dt}\left(\dfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot\theta}\right) = \dfrac{1}{3} m L^2 \ddot\theta$$
- $$\dfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} = - m g \frac{L}{2}(-\sin\theta) = m g \frac{L}{2}\sin\theta.$$
- Therefore Euler–Lagrange yields:
  
  $$
  
  \frac{1}{3} m L^2 \ddot\theta - m g \frac{L}{2}\sin\theta = \tau_{\text{ext}}.
  
  $$
  
  Rearrange:
  
  $$
  
  \tau_{\text{ext}} = \frac{1}{3} m L^2 \ddot\theta - \frac{1}{2} m g L \sin\theta. 
  
  $$
  
  This sign convention appears slightly different from the Newton–Euler version in the sign of the gravity term depending on how \theta is measured and the sign convention for \tau. To reconcile with Newton–Euler form derived earlier, choose consistent sign convention; for instance if positive torque tends to increase \theta and gravity produces torque -\tfrac12 m g L\sin\theta then write:
  
  $$
  
  \tau = \frac{1}{3} m L^2 \ddot\theta + \frac{1}{2} m g L \sin\theta,
  
  $$
  
  or (depending on \theta reference) the Lagrangian equation may present the gravity term with opposite sign. The magnitude and functional dependence are identical; only sign depends on chosen coordinate orientation.
  
  **Conclusion:** Both Newton–Euler and Lagrangian methods produce the same equation of motion (modulo sign conventions):
  
  $$
  
  \frac{1}{3} m L^2 \ddot\theta + \frac{1}{2} m g L \sin\theta = \tau\
  
  $$
  
  (where \tau sign is chosen consistently).
